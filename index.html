Voltage

Voltage is the electrical pressure that pushes electric current through a conductor. It's measured in volts (V) and can be thought of as the "force" behind electricity.

Key Points:

  • Standard household voltage in the US is 120 volts for most outlets
  • Large appliances (dryers, ovens, AC units) use 240 volts
  • Voltage is like water pressure in a pipe - higher voltage means more potential to do work
  • The symbol for voltage is "V" or "E" in electrical formulas

Real-World Example:

Think of voltage like the height of a waterfall. The higher the waterfall (voltage), the more potential energy the water (electricity) has to do work when it falls.

Ohm's Law:

V = I × R

Where V = Voltage, I = Current (Amps), R = Resistance

Amps (Amperage)

Amperage (or current) measures the amount of electrical charge flowing through a circuit per second. It's measured in amperes or amps (A).

Key Points:

  • Amps measure the flow rate of electricity
  • Most household circuits are rated for 15 or 20 amps
  • Heavy-duty appliances may require circuits rated for 30-50 amps
  • Too much amperage can overheat wires and cause fires

Real-World Example:

If voltage is like water pressure, then amperage is like the amount of water flowing through the pipe. A garden hose might flow 5 gallons per minute, while a fire hose flows much more.

Common Amp Ratings:

  • Phone charger: 1-2 amps
  • Light bulb: 0.5-1 amp
  • Microwave: 10-15 amps
  • Electric dryer: 30 amps

Resistance

Resistance is the opposition to the flow of electric current. It's measured in ohms (Ω) and determines how much current will flow for a given voltage.

Key Points:

  • Resistance converts electrical energy into heat
  • Different materials have different resistance levels
  • Copper wire has low resistance (good conductor)
  • Rubber and plastic have high resistance (good insulators)
  • Longer wires have more resistance than shorter wires

Real-World Example:

Resistance is like friction in a water pipe. A smooth, wide pipe (low resistance) allows water to flow easily, while a narrow, rough pipe (high resistance) restricts flow and creates heat from friction.

Practical Applications:

  • Heating elements in toasters and ovens use high resistance to generate heat
  • Dimmer switches add resistance to reduce light brightness
  • Wire gauge is chosen to minimize resistance and prevent overheating

Circuit Breakers

Circuit breakers are safety devices that automatically shut off electrical power when too much current flows through a circuit, preventing overheating and fires.

Key Points:

  • Replace old-style fuses in modern electrical systems
  • Automatically "trip" (turn off) when current exceeds the rated capacity
  • Can be reset manually after tripping
  • Common ratings: 15A, 20A, 30A, 40A, 50A
  • Protect wiring from overheating and potential fires

How They Work:

Circuit breakers contain a metal strip that heats up and bends when too much current flows through it. When it bends far enough, it trips a switch that breaks the circuit, stopping the flow of electricity.

⚠️ Safety Warning:

If a circuit breaker trips repeatedly, it indicates a serious problem. Never replace a breaker with a higher-rated one without upgrading the wiring. This could cause wires to overheat without the breaker tripping.

Types of Circuit Breakers:

  • Single-pole: 120V circuits (15-20A)
  • Double-pole: 240V circuits (30-50A)
  • GFCI breakers: Ground fault protection
  • AFCI breakers: Arc fault protection (required in bedrooms)

Circuit Breaker Panel

The circuit breaker panel (also called a breaker box, load center, or service panel) is the central distribution point for all electrical circuits in your home.

Key Points:

  • Usually located in a basement, garage, or utility room
  • Contains the main breaker and individual circuit breakers
  • Main breaker typically rated for 100, 150, or 200 amps
  • Each circuit breaker controls power to specific areas or appliances
  • Should be labeled to identify which breaker controls which circuit

Panel Components:

  • Main Breaker: Controls power to entire house (can shut off all electricity)
  • Bus Bars: Distribute power from main breaker to individual circuits
  • Branch Breakers: Individual breakers for specific circuits
  • Neutral Bar: Collection point for neutral wires
  • Ground Bar: Collection point for ground wires

⚠️ Safety First:

Never work inside a breaker panel unless you're a licensed electrician. Even with the main breaker off, some parts remain energized and can deliver a fatal shock.

Typical Service Sizes:

  • 100 amps: Older or smaller homes
  • 150 amps: Medium-sized homes
  • 200 amps: Larger homes or homes with electric heating
  • 400 amps: Very large homes with extensive electrical needs

Transformer

A transformer is a device that changes the voltage of electricity. In residential systems, transformers step down high-voltage power from utility lines to the 120/240V used in homes.

Key Points:

  • Power companies transmit electricity at high voltages (thousands of volts)
  • Transformers on utility poles step down voltage for residential use
  • Typical transformation: 7,200V down to 240V/120V
  • Work through electromagnetic induction (no moving parts)
  • Small transformers also used in devices like phone chargers

How Transformers Work:

Transformers use two coils of wire wound around an iron core. Alternating current in the first coil creates a changing magnetic field, which induces voltage in the second coil. The ratio of wire turns determines the voltage change.

Types of Transformers:

  • Step-Down Transformer: Reduces voltage (utility pole transformers)
  • Step-Up Transformer: Increases voltage (at power plants)
  • Isolation Transformer: Provides electrical isolation for safety
  • Auto-Transformer: Single winding used for small voltage adjustments

Why High Voltage Transmission?

Power companies use high voltage for long-distance transmission because it reduces energy loss. Higher voltage means lower current for the same power, and lower current means less heat loss in the wires.

Kilowatt (kW)

A kilowatt is a unit of power equal to 1,000 watts. It measures the rate at which electrical energy is being used or produced at any given moment.

Key Points:

  • 1 kilowatt = 1,000 watts
  • Measures power (rate of energy use), not total energy consumed
  • Formula: Power (W) = Voltage (V) × Current (A)
  • Common appliance ratings are given in watts or kilowatts

Power Calculation:

P = V × I

Power (Watts) = Voltage (Volts) × Current (Amps)

Example: A 120V circuit with 10A current = 1,200W = 1.2 kW

Common Appliance Power Ratings:

  • LED light bulb: 10-15 watts
  • Laptop computer: 50-100 watts
  • Refrigerator: 100-400 watts
  • Microwave: 1,000-1,500 watts (1-1.5 kW)
  • Central AC unit: 3,000-5,000 watts (3-5 kW)
  • Electric dryer: 3,000-5,000 watts (3-5 kW)

Real-World Example:

If you have a 1.5 kW space heater, it draws 1,500 watts of power. On a 120V circuit, that's 12.5 amps (1,500W ÷ 120V = 12.5A).

Kilowatt Hours (kWh)

A kilowatt-hour measures the total amount of electrical energy consumed over time. It's the unit used by power companies to bill customers.

Key Points:

  • 1 kWh = using 1 kilowatt of power for 1 hour
  • Measures energy consumption, not power
  • Your electric bill charges per kWh used
  • Formula: Energy (kWh) = Power (kW) × Time (hours)

Energy Calculation:

Energy (kWh) = Power (kW) × Time (hours)

Example: A 100-watt (0.1 kW) light bulb used for 10 hours = 1 kWh

Calculating Costs:

If electricity costs $0.12 per kWh and you run a 1,500-watt (1.5 kW) space heater for 8 hours:

  • Energy used: 1.5 kW × 8 hours = 12 kWh
  • Cost: 12 kWh × $0.12 = $1.44
  • Monthly cost (30 days): $1.44 × 30 = $43.20

Average Household Consumption:

  • Small apartment: 500-700 kWh/month
  • Average home: 900-1,000 kWh/month
  • Large home: 1,500-2,000+ kWh/month

Understanding Your Electric Bill:

Your electric meter tracks total kWh used. The utility company reads the meter monthly and charges based on consumption. Most bills show:

  • Previous meter reading
  • Current meter reading
  • Total kWh used (difference)
  • Rate per kWh
  • Total charges plus taxes and fees

Common Household Outlet Types

American homes use various types of electrical outlets designed for different voltage requirements and safety features.

Standard Outlet Types:

1. 15A/20A, 120V Duplex Outlet (NEMA 5-15R / 5-20R)

The most common outlet in American homes.

  • Voltage: 120V
  • Amperage: 15 or 20 amps
  • Slots: Two vertical slots (one larger) and a round ground hole
  • Uses: General purpose - lamps, TVs, computers, small appliances
  • Note: 20A outlets have a T-shaped neutral slot

2. GFCI Outlet (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter)

Safety outlet required in wet locations.

  • Features: Test and Reset buttons
  • Required locations: Bathrooms, kitchens, garages, outdoors, basements
  • Function: Shuts off power in milliseconds if ground fault detected
  • Protection: Prevents electrocution in wet conditions

3. 240V Outlets (NEMA 6 and 14 series)

Used for large appliances requiring higher voltage.

  • 30A Dryer Outlet (NEMA 14-30R): 4 prongs, used for electric dryers
  • 50A Range Outlet (NEMA 14-50R): 4 prongs, used for electric ranges/ovens
  • Configuration: Two hot wires, one neutral, one ground
  • Voltage: 240V between hot wires, 120V from each hot to neutral

4. AFCI Outlet (Arc Fault Circuit Interrupter)

Protects against electrical arcs that can cause fires.

  • Required: In bedrooms and living areas (newer construction)
  • Function: Detects dangerous arcing conditions
  • Features: Similar appearance to GFCI with test/reset buttons

5. USB Outlets

Modern outlets with built-in USB charging ports.

  • Features: Standard outlets plus USB-A or USB-C ports
  • Convenience: Charge devices without adapters
  • Popular locations: Bedrooms, kitchens, home offices

⚠️ Safety Reminders:

  • Never force a plug into an outlet that doesn't fit
  • Replace cracked or damaged outlets immediately
  • Don't overload outlets with too many devices
  • Use outlet covers in homes with small children
  • Test GFCI outlets monthly using the test button

GFI (GFCI) Circuit

GFCI stands for Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (also called GFI). It's a life-saving device that protects against electrical shock by detecting ground faults and shutting off power almost instantly.

Key Points:

  • Required by electrical code in wet locations
  • Detects current imbalances as small as 4-6 milliamps
  • Trips (shuts off) in as little as 1/40th of a second
  • Can be a GFCI outlet, GFCI breaker, or portable GFCI device
  • Must be tested monthly to ensure proper operation

How GFCI Works:

A GFCI constantly monitors the current flowing through the hot and neutral wires. In a normal circuit, these currents should be equal. If current is "leaking" to ground (such as through a person touching water), the GFCI detects this imbalance and immediately cuts power.

Example: If you drop a hair dryer in water, the GFCI detects the current flowing through the water to ground and shuts off power before you can be electrocuted.

Required GFCI Locations:

  • Bathrooms: All outlets within 6 feet of sinks or tubs
  • Kitchens: Outlets serving countertop areas
  • Garages: All outlets (except dedicated appliances)
  • Outdoors: All outdoor outlets
  • Basements: Unfinished basements
  • Laundry areas: Near sinks
  • Wet bars: Outlets within 6 feet of sinks

Testing Your GFCI:

  1. Press the TEST button on the GFCI outlet
  2. The RESET button should pop out and power should shut off
  3. Test with a nightlight or outlet tester to confirm power is off
  4. Press the RESET button to restore power
  5. If the GFCI doesn't trip when tested, replace it immediately

Test monthly to ensure protection.

⚠️ Important Safety Information:

  • GFCI protection is required by code in wet locations
  • A non-functioning GFCI provides no protection - replace immediately
  • GFCI outlets can protect downstream outlets when wired correctly
  • Don't confuse GFCI with surge protectors - they serve different purposes
  • If a GFCI trips frequently, there may be a wiring problem or faulty appliance

GFCI vs. Circuit Breaker:

  • Circuit Breaker: Protects against overloads and short circuits (fires)
  • GFCI: Protects against ground faults (electrocution)
  • Both are needed for complete protection
  • A circuit breaker won't trip fast enough to prevent electrocution in many cases
